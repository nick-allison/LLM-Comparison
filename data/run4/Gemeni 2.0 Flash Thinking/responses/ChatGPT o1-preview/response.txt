The Monty Hall problem is a probability puzzle based on a game show scenario. You choose one of three doors, behind one is a car, and behind the other two are goats. After you choose, the host, who knows where the car is, opens one of the doors you didn't choose, revealing a goat. He then asks if you want to switch to the other unopened door.

Switching doors doubles your probability of winning the car. Initially, you have a 1/3 chance of picking the door with the car. This means there is a 2/3 chance the car is behind one of the other two doors. When the host opens a door to reveal a goat, he is giving you information. He is not changing the initial 1/3 probability of your chosen door having the car, but he is concentrating the 2/3 probability onto the remaining unopened door. By switching, you are taking advantage of that initial 2/3 probability.

Correlation means two variables are related or tend to move together. Causation means one variable directly causes a change in another. Correlation does not imply causation because a third, unobserved variable (a confounding variable) could be influencing both variables, creating a correlation without a direct causal link.

Example: Ice cream sales and crime rates are positively correlated. However, ice cream sales do not cause crime, nor does crime cause ice cream sales. Both are likely influenced by a third variable, temperature. Warmer temperatures lead to increased ice cream sales and also tend to be associated with higher crime rates.

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic example in game theory that demonstrates why two purely "rational" individuals might not cooperate, even if it appears that it is in their best interests to do so.

Two suspects are arrested for a crime and placed in separate cells. They cannot communicate with each other. Each is given the choice to either betray the other (defect) or cooperate with the other (remain silent). The possible outcomes are:

* **Both cooperate (remain silent):** Both receive a light sentence (e.g., 1 year each).
* **One defects, the other cooperates:** The defector goes free, and the cooperator receives a heavy sentence (e.g., 5 years).
* **Both defect (betray each other):** Both receive a moderate sentence (e.g., 3 years each).

From an individual perspective, regardless of what the other prisoner does, it's always better to defect. If the other prisoner cooperates, defecting leads to freedom instead of a light sentence. If the other prisoner defects, defecting leads to a moderate sentence instead of a heavy sentence.  However, if both prisoners follow this "rational" logic and defect, they both end up with a worse outcome (moderate sentence) than if they had both cooperated (light sentence).

The Prisoner's Dilemma illustrates the conflict between individual rationality and collective rationality, and it has broad applications in fields like economics, political science, and evolutionary biology to understand situations where cooperation is beneficial but difficult to achieve.
